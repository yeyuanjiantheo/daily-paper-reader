## Abstract 

In Online Convex Optimization (OCO), when the stochastic gradient has a finite 1

variance, many algorithms provably work and guarantee a sublinear regret. How-2

ever, limited results are known if the gradient estimate has a heavy tail, i.e., the 3

stochastic gradient only admits a finite p-th central moment for some p ‚àà (1 , 2] .4

Motivated by it, this work examines different old algorithms for OCO (e.g., Online 5

Gradient Descent) in the more challenging heavy-tailed setting. Under the standard 6

bounded domain assumption, we establish new regrets for these classical methods 7

without any algorithmic modification. Remarkably, these regret bounds are fully 8

optimal in all parameters (can be achieved even without knowing p), suggesting 9

that OCO with heavy tails can be solved effectively without any extra operation 10 

(e.g., gradient clipping). Our new results have several applications. A particularly 11 

interesting one is the first provable convergence result for nonsmooth nonconvex 12 

optimization under heavy-tailed noise without gradient clipping. 13

However, many works have pointed out that even for the easier stochastic optimization (i.e., ‚Ñìt = F35

challenging when p < 2. A particular evidence is that the famous Stochastic Gradient Descent ( SGD )40 

algorithm [32] (which is exactly OGD for stochastic optimization) provably diverges [45]. 41 

Though heavy-tailed stochastic optimization has been extensively studied [ 18 , 26 , 34 ], limited results 42 

are known for OCO with heavy tails. The only work under this topic that we are aware of is [ 47 ], 43 

which established a parameter-free regret bound in high probability (more discussions provided 44 

later). However, their algorithm includes many nontrivial modifications like gradient clipping and 45 

significantly deviates from the existing simple OCO algorithms used in practice. Especially, consider 46 

OGD as an example. Though the heavy-tailed issue is known, OGD (or just think of it as SGD ) still 47 

works (sometimes very well) in practice even without gradient clipping and is arguably one of the 48 

most popular optimizers, which seemingly contradicts the theory of unconvergence mentioned before. 49 

This indicates that, for classical OCO algorithms under heavy-tailed noise, a huge gap exists between 50 

the empirical convergence (or even the effective practical performance) and theoretical guarantees. 51 

Therefore, we are naturally led to the following question: 52 

In what context can old OCO algorithms work under heavy tails, in what sense, and to what extent? 53

We emphasize that the motivation behind [ 47 ] differs heavily from ours. They aim to solve heavy-83

gradient clipping, artificially added regularization, and solving the additional fixed-point equation. 85 

However, their result cannot reflect why the existing simple OCO algorithms like OGD work in 86 

practice under heavy-tailed noise. In contrast, our goal is to examine whether, when, and how the 87

Moreover, we would like to mention two drawbacks of [ 47 ]. First, though the T 1/p regret seems 89

This suggests that their bound is not entirely optimal. Second, we remark that they require knowing 92

to satisfy in the online setting. In comparison, our regret bound GD ‚àöT + œÉDT 1/p is fully optimal 94 

in all parameters. Importantly, AdaGrad can achieve it while oblivious to the problem information. 95

## 3 Old Algorithms under Heavy Tails 118 

In this section, we revisit three classical algorithms for OCO: OGD , DA , and AdaGrad , whose regret 119 

bounds are well-studied in the finite variance case but remain unknown under heavy-tailed noise. 120 

The basic idea of proving these algorithms work under heavy tails is to leverage the boundness 121 

property of X . We will describe it in more detail using OGD as an illustrated example. The analysis 122 

of DA follows a similar way at a high level, but differs in some details. However, though AdaGrad 123 

can be viewed as OGD with an adaptive stepsize, the way to utilize the boundness property is entirely 124 

different. All formal proofs are deferred to the appendix due to space limitations. 125

## 5 Conclusion and Limitation 313 

This paper shows that three classical OCO algorithms, OGD , DA , and AdaGrad , can achieve the 314 

optimal in-expectation regret under heavy tails without any algorithmic modification if the feasible 315 

set is bounded, and provides some applications in stochastic optimization. The main limitation of 316 

our work is that all the proof crucially relies on the bounded domain assumption, which may not 317 

always be suitable in practice. Finding a weaker sufficient condition, under which the classical OCO 318 

algorithms work with heavy tails provably, is a direction worth studying in the future. 319
