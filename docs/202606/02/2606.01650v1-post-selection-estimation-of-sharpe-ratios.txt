# Post-Selection Estimation of Sharpe Ratios 

Steven E. Pav ∗

June 2, 2026 

Abstract 

We consider the problem of estimating the true Sharpe ratio of an as-set selected for having the highest observed in-sample Sharpe ratio among many assets. We discuss estimators based on the polyhedral lemma, James Stein shrinkage, debiasing the expected maximum Sharpe ratio, thresholding and empirical Bayes. We test these estimators in simula-tions, computing bias and root mean square error across different values of sample size, number of assets, and spread and shape of population Sharpe ratios. We also compute rank correlation of the estimators against the underlying quantity, simulating how these estimators might be used to compare or rank the output of different teams which perform this se-lection process. We find that the James Stein estimator provides the best performance across many different realistic values of the relevant parame-ters, followed by the GMLEB estimator of Jiang and Zhang. These results are fairly robust to correlation of asset returns, with some caveats. 

# 1 Introduction 

From the dawn of computing, quantitative-minded traders have used comput-ers to try to analyze and predict market movements. [14, 3] If the quality of outcomes was monotonic in computing power invested, however, there would be many rich nerds indeed. This seems not to be the case, instead devising and backtesting thousands or millions of candidate trading strategies often results in disappointing performance out of sample. There is a clear need even still for statistical tools to correct for the effects of selecting strategies based on their in-sample performance. The setup we consider is fairly simple: a quantitative strategist devises and backtests some large number of strategies, selects the one with the highest in-sample Sharpe ratio, then wishes to estimate or perform inference on the popu-lation Sharpe ratio of that strategy. Early inferential approaches to the problem relied on corrections for familywise error rate. [2, 4] White proposed the use of the bootstrap for his “Reality Check”, which was improved upon by Hansen 

inter alia . [23, 8] Previously we compared several procedures for the inferential task, including approaches based on directional alternatives and a conditional test based on the “polyhedral lemma” which finds currency in the field of selec-tive inference. [17, 21, 15] More recent approaches leverage statistical learning theory for the inference problem. [16]

In this work we consider the estimation problem, namely: conditional on selecting an asset or strategy because it has the highest in-sample Sharpe ratio, can we compute an estimate of the signal-noise ratio of that asset or strategy which is unbiased or has low mean square error? Outside the context of quan-titative trading, this problem has considerable history. One approach to the problem is via thresholding , where values above a threshold (perhaps chosen empirically) are shrunk by a certain amount towards zero. [6, 13, 12] This can also be viewed from the viewpoint of Empirical Bayes. [7] Reid et al. introduced a maximum likelihood estimator based on the polyhedral lemma, and compared it to several other techniques, including the threshold-based methods, as well as the James-Stein estimator. [19, 9] This note does not seek to introduce new methods for the problem. Rather we adapt existing methods to the problem of estimating the signal-noise ratio of the asset with maximum Sharpe ratio, and test them on population parameters which are likely to be of interest to practitioners, with realistic sample sizes, effect sizes and distribution and number of strategies considered. In that regard, this paper is quite similar to that of Reid et al., but adapted to the econometrics of the Sharpe ratio. [19]

# 3 Simulations 

We perform a number of Monte Carlo simulations of the strategy backtest-select-estimate pipeline we describe above. We evaluate the different estimators in two different ways: in one we simply compute the bias and root mean square error (RMSE) of the estimator compared to ζk; in the other we simulate using one of these estimators to select from several different sources of strategies, and compute the correlation of the estimators against the ζk.At its heart a single simulation consists of the following: 51. Either the ζ is given, or it is generated according to some distribution; when randomly generated, we usually control the σ2 

> ζ

.2. We then generate n days of independent normally distributed returns for 

k assets with zero mean and unit variance. 3. To each column we add the value of ζ so each column is a n-vector with signal-noise ratio equal to the corresponding element of ζ.4. We compute the Sharpe ratio of each column. 5. We apply the various estimators to the vector ˆζ and record them. We also record the ζk.We typically perform many simulations for each setting of the ζ, n and k.Over those many simulations we compute the bias and RMSE of the estimator compared to the signal-noise ratio of the strategy selected for having the highest Sharpe ratio. We then plot the bias or RMSE versus, say, the σ2 

> ζ

or n and so on. 

Forms of the population vector We consider a number of different layouts for the vector ζ. Some of these are random and the ζ is generated afresh for each simulation. In others the vector ζ is fixed at one value across many simulations. The layouts are: 

• Gaussian: The ζ is drawn from a Gaussian distribution with mean zero and variance σ2 

> ζ

.

• Uniform: The ζ is drawn from a continuous uniform distribution with mean zero and variance σ2 

> ζ

. That is, elements of ζ are drawn uniformly from −√3σζ to √3σζ .

• Bimodal: The ζ takes value ±σζ with probability 12 .

• all-good: The ζ equals ζ01 for some ζ0. This is a non-random configura-tion, constant across all simulations with this layout. We test this layout with various values of ζ0 mainly as a check that our estimators are location invariant. 

• one-good: One element of ζ is ζ0 while the rest are −ζ0 for some ζ0.This is a non-random configuration. When ζ0 is relatively large compared to n−1/2 we are likely to select the “good” strategy, but some of our estimators are likely to be very biased. 

• two-good: Two elements of ζ are ζ0 while the rest are −ζ0 for some ζ0.This is a non-random configuration. 

Estimators We consider the following estimators: 

• Biased: This is ˆζk, the maximal Sharpe ratio of all those observed. We include this as a benchmark for the alternative estimators. 

• Grand Mean: This is ¯ζ, the average of all the observed Sharpe ratio values. 

• James-Stein: The James-Stein estimator, shrunk towards the grand mean. 

• GMLEB: The estimator of Jiang and Zhang shrunk to the grand mean. 

• SURE: The estimator of Donoho and Johnstone shrunk to the grand mean. 

• Polyhedral Median: This is the 0.5 confidence bound based on the poly-hedral lemma. 

• Polyhedral MLE: The MLE estimator of Reid et al. 

• Expected Max: This is ˆζk minus the expected value of the maximum found by Bailey et al. 6• Empirical Bayes: The estimator of Johnstone and Silverman, shrunk to the grand mean. We use the code from the EbayesThresh R package. [22] 

3.1 Bias and Square Error Results 

First we performed simulations using the Gaussian, Uniform and Bimodal dis-tributions for ζ. In these we let σζ range from 0yr −1/2 to 1yr −1/2. We let k

take values 10, 100, 1000. We varied n to represent between 2 and 8 years at 252 days per year. For each setting we perform 500 simulations. In Figure 1 we plot the bias versus σζ , and in Figure 2 we plot the RMSE versus σζ , fixing n

at 4 years, and plotting for the various values of k tested. Based on early results of these studies, we removed the Polyhedral Median and MLE estimators from the bias and RMSE plots. Both consistently exhibited high RMSE, much higher than the other estimators. The issue arises when the ˆζk and ˆζk−1 are very close to each other; in this case the truncated normal that arises from the polyhedral lemma is very close to its limiting value which causes very extreme values of the estimators, see Section B for more details. For small numbers of simulations you might not hit such a case, but we did when performing 500 simulations. We will consider these two estimators in the following subsection, but will see they perform poorly there as well. In those plots we see that the Biased estimator is indeed biased, and often has the highest RMSE. The Grand Mean is unbiased and efficient when σζ = 0, but otherwise negatively biased and has high RMSE. The James Stein estimator is, to the resolution of the plot, effectively unbiased for the Gaussian layout and has the lowest RMSE in that case as well; when ζ is uniform or bimodal, James Stein exhibits some positive bias and increased RMSE for larger σζ . GMLEB is slightly positively biased for uniform and bimodal ζ, but appears to have RMSE which is flat with respect to ζ. The Expected Max estimator exhibits negative bias for Gaussian ζ, but is better behaved for uniform and bimodal layouts. In Figure 3 we plot the bias versus σζ , and in Figure 4 we plot the RMSE versus σζ for these simulations, but fixing k = 100 and varying n in the different facet rows. This shows how error depends on the sample size when the number of strategies is fixed. The middle row of Figure 3 should be the same as the middle row of Figure 1, and similarly for Figure 4 and Figure 2. One thing to observe in these plots is how the estimators respond to varying n: the Grand Mean seems almost unaffected, which makes sense; most of the other estimators appear to have less bias for larger n, but this is tricky to quantify. In Figure 5 we reverse this relationship and plot the RMSE versus n in years with lines for selected values of σζ . We can see more clearly here that all of the methods seem to improve (have lower RMSE) with increasing sample size except for the Grand Mean, and perhaps the James Stein method for bimodal layout and large σζ .How can we make sense of the various plots above? The simulations above looked at the quality of estimators qua estimators of ζk.We wish to also examine how these estimators can be used to compare two or more selected strategies, each found by the process of selecting based on Sharpe ratio. As an illustration, suppose that former cryptographers Alice and Bob are now developing quantitative strategies. Alice is adventurous : she has many different ideas and performs many backtests. Bob is boring : he tests a few vari-ants on one idea. Both follow our process of generating some ideas, backtesting them, then selecting the one with the highest in-sample Sharpe ratio. Depend-ing on the mean signal-noise ratio of their idea generating processes, their σ2 

> ζ

,the n and k, either Alice or Bob might select a better strategy. We wish an estimator computed on their respective ˆζ vectors to be directionally correct for the signal-noise ratio of each of their chosen strategies. That is to say a good estimator would be one which is likely to identify the participant with higher 

ζk.To measure the association of the estimator and the population value ζk,we perform a number of simulations across different configurations. We then compute Kendall’s rank correlation coefficient, τ of an estimate against the population value. This gives some idea of the agreement of pairwise rankings of the two quantities. We also compute Spearman’s rank correlation coefficient, ρ

In the simulations considered heretofore in this note, the returns of assets were independent, that is R = I. We wish to consider how these estimators will perform for more general correlation structures. The simplest model, and one which is fairly accurate for a lot of quantitative work, is that of equicorrelation or compound symmetric structure where R = (1 − ρ) I + ρ11 ⊤. In our simulations we treat ρ as another knob to be controlled. We do not modify the estimators in any way to deal with correlation, rather we treat the correlation as a nuisance. We note that in the extreme, for ρ ≈ 1.0, the errors in ˆζ are all perfectly aligned, and the ordering of the ˆζ will mirror that of ζ. That is, with high probability ζk will be the largest element of ζ. In this case there is no selection bias from selecting the asset based on the Sharpe ratio, and the Biased estimator should come to dominate. We note that the estimators based on the polyhedral lemma have no actual dependence on the correlation structure, cf. the additional analysis in Section B in the appendix. Thus we expect no real degradation of (otherwise poor) performance for the Polyhedral Median and Polyhedral MLE estimators. Towards this end we perform simulations to estimate the bias and RMSE of the various estimators. As above, we perform simulations using three layouts of 

ζ. We fix σζ at 0 .5yr −1/2, n = 1008, k = 100. We let ρ vary from 0 .05 to 0 .95. For each setting of the parameters we perform 500 simulations. In Figure 12 we plot the bias versus ρ; in Figure 13 we plot the RMSE. As perhaps expected, as ρ → 1, the bias of all estimators becomes negative, except the Biased estimator which becomes unbiased. All the serious estimators are very biased for large ρ, though perhaps GMLEB has the best performance for correlated asset returns. In terms of RMSE, all estimators show increased RMSE as ρ → 1, except the Biased estimator. Likely much of this is due to the increased bias. Based on the bias, RMSE and correlation studies, we recommend the use of the James Stein estimator both as an estimator and for comparing potential investment strategies. The James Stein estimator is also recommended for its relative simplicity: it can be computed knowing only k, n, ˆζk, ¯ζ and the empirical variance of the ˆζ. If one were somehow certain that there were only a few good strategies among all strategies tested, we might recommend GMLEB, but the code is a bit more complicated. We cannot recommend the estimators based on the polyhedral lemma, nor the Expected Max estimator, due to poor empirical performance. These results are fairly robust against correlation of asset returns. If one needed a general purpose estimator for ranking selected strategies, then James Stein should be used if the number of strategies k is always fixed, or if n is fixed and fairly large. If one were certain that returns were highly correlated with each other, however, the Biased estimator is recommended as it is effectively unbiased! We consider this study still preliminary, as there are obvious improvements that could be made: 1. In our analysis we viewed correlation of returns as a nuisance that threat-ens the robustness of our findings, when in reality correlation is an unfortu-nate fact of life. Some of the methods considered have obvious extensions to the case of correlated returns, but a practical estimator would have to estimate the common correlation, or any other adjustments required of a more general R. We have not implemented those procedures yet, but anticipate doing so in a future revision of this paper. 2. One can easily imagine some of the estimators could be improved by elim-inating irrelevant elements of ˆζ. That is we would seek some way of choosing a threshold so that we ignore all tested strategies with Sharpe ratio less than ˆζk − δ for some δ depending on n. Perhaps such a thresh-old could be chosen using Hansen’s log-log trick. [17, 8] In particular we suspect this might improve the less selective estimators like James Stein and Expected Max. 3. Many of the estimators considered here were constructed for the problem of reduced MSE estimation of the entire vector ζ, not for the selective case of estimating ζk. Perhaps they can be further tuned to reduce MSE for our problem. In particular it seems the SURE estimate could be improved perhaps by clever application of Stein’s lemma to the truncated Gaussian conditional distribution we get from the polyhedral lemma. 4. There is likely a way to look at ˆζ and route it to one of the estimators considered here, or otherwise ensemble a few of the estimators in a way that has even better RMSE. 5. Estimators based on the polyhedral lemma are unstable when ˆζk ≈ ˆζk−1.A better approach is needed for selective inference, one which recognizes that if ˆζk−1 were just a little bigger we would be considering that strategy instead. Such an approach would likely give tighter confidence intervals for the inferential problem as well. [17] 6. We plan to release the code for this paper as open source, after cleaning it up. If you have an estimator you think is better, or you think we straw-manned your favorite estimator by our choice of testing parameters, by 29 all means re-run the code yourself.
