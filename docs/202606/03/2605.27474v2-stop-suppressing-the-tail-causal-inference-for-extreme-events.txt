# Stop Suppressing the Tail: Causal Inference for Extreme Events

eichi.uehara@aflo.one

Abstract 

Estimating how an outcome responds, on average, to a continuous treatment — the Average Dose-Response Function (ADRF) — is a core causal-inference primitive. When outcomes are heavy-tailed (financial returns, climate losses, drug-dosage adverse events), standard robust double machine learning (DML) deliberately suppresses the tail to stabilise the bulk average. Yet in exactly these high-stakes settings the tail is the target quantity: the 

1-in-1000 loss, not the bulk mean, determines the decision, so a bulk-only estimate omits the quantity of interest. A natural alternative — reading the tail off the model’s residu-als — is circular: the inferred Generalised-Pareto shape ̂ ξ shifts from −0.42 (bounded) to 

+0 .28 (heavy) on one dataset solely as a function of switching the core estimator between Huber and Welsch. We propose an ADRF estimator that emits a structured tail-shape output alongside the point estimate, without suppressing the extremes. Its tail diagnostic (PDHTE+JK) estimates the per-treatment tail shape from the outcome centred by a pilot median — not from core-estimator residuals — which breaks the circular dependence and renders the diagnostic invariant to the choice of core method. The output comprises four quantities: treatment-conditional tail shape ̂ ξ(t), deep-tail return levels ̂ Qα(t), conditional shortfalls ̂ Sα(t), the recovered mean ADRF, and an explicit refusal that declines extrap-olation when the data do not support extreme-value modelling. Against kernel-weighted quantile regression (QR), the proposed estimator reduces deep-tail ( α = 0 .001 ) return-level MAE by 11% and conditional-shortfall MAE by 25 .5% across a 10 -DGP heavy-tailed panel, and by 20 –29% in the sample-scarce regime ( n ≤ 2000 ). On real freMTPL2 motor-insurance claims (excess kurtosis 1667 ) it explicitly refuses on the log-claim scale — an output neither QR nor loss-only DML produces. 

Keywords: double machine learning, dose-response, heavy tails, extreme value theory, causal inference. 

# 1 Introduction 

The Average Dose-Response Function (ADRF) θ(t) = E[Y (t)] under continuous treatment is the standard estimand of double machine learning (6, 7, 30). When the conditional distribution of Y has a heavy tail with shape ξ (in the Pickands–Balkema–de Haan sense (8)), θ(t) is either 

undefined (ξ ≥ 1: the mean does not exist) or estimated at a sub-√n rate (ξ ∈ [0 .5, 1) : infinite variance, sample mean converges at n1/2−ξ). Heavy-tail residuals are ubiquitous in financial returns, insurance claims, climate-loss panels, and any setting where the outcome aggregates rare extreme events. The standard remedy is robust DML — replace the squared-error loss of the second-stage local-linear smoother with a bounded-influence loss (Huber, Quantile, Winsor) (20–22). Robust DML is empirically competitive, but it has two persistent shortcomings: 

• Tail-blind tuning. Bounded-influence losses have tuning constants (Huber’s clipping ϵ,Tukey’s biweight c) fixed by loss design, not by the data’s actual tail. A Huber-DML with 

ϵ = 1 .35 clips at the same residual scale on Gaussian noise and on Pareto-1.5 contamination. 1

• No tail output. The robust loss is designed to suppress the tail and emits no information about the suppressed component — neither a tail-shape estimate, a probability of exceedance, nor a regret-aware extreme-quantile prediction. Only the bulk-stabilised point estimate is returned; the tail quantities of practical interest (rare-claim severity, extreme-loss quantile, worst-case dose) are omitted. We propose a two-component estimator that addresses both shortcomings without abandon-ing the strengths of robust DML (Figure 1).

# 2 Background and Related Work 

We organise prior work along the four axes our estimator combines: DML for the ADRF, robust second-stage M-estimators, peaks-over-threshold inference with the GPD, and extreme-tail conformal and causal estimands. Under unconfoundedness, positivity, and SUTVA, θ(t) = E[Y (t)] = EX [E[Y | T = t, X ]] 

is identified. Cross-fit DML (6) fits, in each of K folds, nuisance estimates of E[Y | X] and 

E[T | X] on the K−1 training folds. The continuous-treatment extension of Colangelo and Lee (7), Semenova and Chernozhukov (30) runs a kernel-weighted local-linear fit (14) of the residual on T at each grid point with Silverman bandwidth. When Var( rY | T = t) fails under a heavy tail, the standard remedy replaces squared-error with a bounded-influence loss in the local linear: Huber (bounded but non-redescending, (20)), Quantile/median (21), Tukey biweight and Welsch (redescending, (3, 22)). All treat the tuning constant as a fixed loss hyperparameter. TailWelsch-DML in this paper makes the Welsch 

γ a function of the data-driven tail shape ̂ ξ.Beyond the bulk, the Pickands–Balkema–de Haan theorem gives convergence to the GPD above high thresholds (8). Threshold-selection families include graphical Mean-Excess / Hill plots (4, 8, 17), sequential goodness-of-fit (1, 24, 25, 33), L-moments/PWM stability (18, 19), and composite-density splice models (29). Our composite-likelihood rule (Definition 1) is a splice-density variant that selects u⋆ by maximising the held-out log-likelihood (a strictly proper scoring rule) with a built-in no-tail fallback. Closer to our setting, adaptive conformal inference (2, 15, 36) and its extreme-regime vari-ants (26, 27, 32) splice a GPD tail above a high quantile of calibration scores. The hybrid empirical-plus-GPD return-level curves of Section 5 are methodologically related, but per-T

(treatment-conditional) on post-DML residuals. Heavy-tail causal estimands have been ad-dressed via propensity-score trimming (9, 12, 35), ATE winsorisation (28), and extremal QTE for binary treatment (11); all act on covariate or propensity axes, whereas TailWelsch-DML 

3operates in the outcome-residual dimension after cross-fit DML. EVT methods in causal infer-ence include the causal-tail coefficient (16), extreme graphical models (13), and conditional-EVT for heteroscedastic financial series (23). None of this literature ties the second-stage loss tuning to a data-driven tail estimate or emits a structured set of tail-conditional quantities alongside the ADRF point estimate.

Proposition 1 (Method-invariance) . PDHTE consumes only (Y, T ) centred by the pilot median, so two consistent core estimators producing different ̂ θ(t) yield identical ̂ ξPDHTE (t) outputs. 

Empirical verification: on the asymmetric DGP where PWM applied to Standard / Huber / Welsch residuals spans −0.42 to +0 .28 , PDHTE refuses in all 5 seeds (Table 2). On regular-varying tails (Pareto, two-Pareto) the PWM consensus is within ±0.05 and PDHTE matches it. Method-invariance establishes that PDHTE’s output does not depend on the choice of core estimator, but it does not by itself imply that the output is a causal quantity. The next step is to relate the observational law that PDHTE consumes to the causal target. PDHTE estimates the tail of the observational law Y | T = t. The causal target is the tail of the potential outcome Y (t), whose law is EX [FY |T =t,X ] under unconfoundedness, whereas Y | T = t has law 

EX|T =t[FY |T =t,X ]. These coincide when T ⊥ X — which holds by construction in the synthetic panel (Section 6: T is drawn independently of X), so every synthetic result estimates the causal tail directly. Two facts govern the confounded ( T̸ ⊥ X) case: 

Proposition 2 (Tail index is confounding-robust) . The tail index of a mixture equals the maxi-mum of the component indices. Hence whenever positivity holds — every X-stratum has positive density of every t — the index of EX|T =t[FY |T =t,X ] equals that of EX [FY |T =t,X ]: confounding shifts mixture proportions but not the maximum. The per-T̂ ξ(t) is therefore a causal quantity even under confounding. 

The return level ̂ Qα(t) and shortfall ̂ Sα(t) depend on mixture proportions and are con-founded. We apply a first-order partial deconfounding : stabilized generalized-propensity-score (GPS) weights sw i =̂ fT (Ti)/̂fT |X (Ti | Xi) multiply the kernel weights, restoring the marginal 7X-distribution in each T -band. Because ̂ fT |X is a treatment-side nuisance shared by all core methods, method-invariance (Proposition 1) is preserved. This GPS correction is not a free lunch. It is consistent only when overlap holds and the propensity is correctly specified, and continuous-treatment IPW is intrinsically high-variance — constraints we now make explicit. The correction requires positivity/overlap ( ̂fT |X bounded away from zero on the reported t-grid) and a correctly specified GPS, here a Gaussian propensity from a cross-fit ̂ E[T | X]. Continuous-treatment IPW is high-variance, amplified in the tail where the weighted quantile rests on few heavily-reweighted exceedances; we winsorise and hard-clip the weights and restrict the t-grid to the adequate-overlap interior. The result is a 

partial correction: Section 6 verifies a 63% reduction in causal-return-level bias, leaving ∼ 37% .We do not claim √n-consistency of the GPS-weighted hybrid estimator; a doubly-robust or trimmed-weight refinement removing the residual bias is future work (Section 7). Taking together the core estimator of Section 1 and the PDHTE diagnostic just defined, the proposed pipeline pairs a redescending Welsch local-linear M-estimator (constant γ = 0 .10 )with PDHTE+JK for per-T̂ ξ(t). The post-core component (Section 5) then emits the five tail-conditional quantities. The design rule is that tail information enters the post-core output, not the loss function — feeding ̂ ξ(t) back into the Welsch γ is empirically worse than a constant 

γ on the 320 -cell panel.

# 7 Discussion 

We have argued that the standard practice of suppressing the heavy tail to stabilise an ADRF point estimate discards the quantity that drives high-stakes decisions, and that reading the tail off post-DML residuals reintroduces the choice of core estimator as a confounder of the tail diagnostic. The proposed estimator answers both concerns: a constant-clipping Welsch core (TailWelsch-DML ) preserves the standard Neyman-orthogonal DML rate, and a plateau-detection diagnostic (PDHTE+JK) on the pilot-median- centred outcome produces a per-T tail shape that is invariant to the choice of core method. The resulting tail-conditional output — treatment-conditional shape, return level, conditional shortfall, sign-conditional mean recovery, and an explicit refusal — is produced at O(n log n) cost on the residuals already computed. Empirically the estimator reduces deep-tail return-level MAE by 11% and conditional-shortfall MAE by 25 .5% against the quantile-regression baseline, and by 20 –29% for n ≤ 2000 ; on freMTPL2 motor-insurance claims it refuses on the log-claim scale, indicating that the data do not support EVT extrapolation. Two scope conditions qualify these results. First, TailWelsch-DML targets a Welsch M-functional that equals E[Y (t)] only under symmetric residuals; under asymmetric contamination it approximates the structural θ(t), and the sign-conditional recovery (Equation (11)) is the additional step for the mean ADRF including the tail. For ξ ≥ 1 the mean is undefined and only the M-functional is sensible. Second, under T -X confounding the tail index remains causal by max-stability (Proposition 2); the return level and shortfall are confounded and are corrected by GPS-stabilised weighting, which removes 63% of the bias but is not √n-consistent. Within this scope, several limitations remain. (i) Per-T̂ ξ(t) retains ∼ 0.05 residual bias after jackknife, carried into ̂ Qα(t) at deep α; ordering across t is reliable below n ∼ 1500 but absolute level is not. (ii) T -localised contamination defeats TailWelsch-DML , Standard, and Huber alike. (iii) Only the conditional shortfall is independently validated against an oracle; the causal-tail effect inherits its accuracy from ̂ Qα but is not separately benchmarked. (iv) The estimator targets univariate residual tails under weak temporal dependence; the Laplace bulk is a swappable default. (v) Three asymptotic gaps remain open: pilot-median error propagation through the DEdH limit (Section 4), GPS-weighted return-level consistency (Section 4), and MSE-optimal jackknife damping (Equation (10)). Closing these and extending to multivariate residual tails are the natural next steps.

B.5 Hyperparameters in TailWelsch-DML 

TailWelsch-DML uses a constant Welsch clipping γ = 0 .10 — a mild redescending down-weighting.
