Abstract 

Modeling uncertainty in heavy-tailed time series remains a critical challenge for deep probabilistic forecasting models, which often struggle to capture abrupt, extreme events. While L√©vy stable distributions offer a natural framework for mod-eling such non-Gaussian behaviors, the intractability of their probability density functions severely limits conventional likelihood-based inference. To address this, we introduce DeepL√©vy, a neural framework that learns mixtures of L√©vy stable distributions by minimizing the discrepancy between empirical and parametric char-acteristic functions. DeepL√©vy incorporates a mixture mechanism that adaptively learns context-dependent weights and parameters over multiple L√©vy components, enabling flexible multi-horizon uncertainty modeling. Evaluations on both real and synthetic datasets demonstrate that DeepL√©vy outperforms state-of-the-art deep probabilistic forecasting approaches in tail risk metrics, especially under extreme volatility. 

1 Introduction 

Forecasting extreme events in time series plays a pivotal role in high-stakes domains such as epidemio-logical crisis management [ 3, 28 ] and financial risk assessment [ 34 ]. Given a historical sequence x1: T ,conventional deep forecasting methods typically train a model f (x1: T ) to approximate the conditional distribution P (yT +1: T +H |x1: T ) over a prediction horizon H. Although recent advancements in deep learning have demonstrated remarkable capabilities in modeling temporal dependencies [ 8, 18 ], effective decision-making, particularly during "black swan" events like market crashes or pandemic surges, requires accurately estimating the tail risks underlying the data [ 25 ]. To address this, many recent studies have pivoted towards probabilistic forecasting [ 9 , 6], aiming to quantify the full range of possible future outcomes rather than merely predicting a deterministic trajectory. Deep neural-based probabilistic forecasting models, such as DeepAR [ 30 ], DeepVAR [ 29 ], DSSM [ 24 ], DeepFactor [ 37 ], ProbTransformer [ 33 ], etc., have gained significant traction due to their ability to output valid probability density functions. However, existing methods predominantly rely on the assumption that the noise ѕµ follows a Gaussian or light-tailed distribution with finite variance [ 12 ]. While effective for routine data, this assumption fundamentally fails in heavy-tailed regimes where the variance may be infinite and the probability of extreme deviations decays polynomially rather than exponentially [ 26 , 27 ]. The primary objective of these models is restricted by the expressiveness of standard parametric families, which cannot capture the infinite variance and extreme asymmetry in-herent in complex real-world systems. To better address the challenges of heavy-tailed and multimodal dynamics, we introduce the 

DeepL√©vy , a novel framework that fundamentally relaxes the finite-variance assumption by modeling the conditional distribution as a mixture of L√©vy ќ±-stable distributions [ 1, 35 ]. Unlike Gaussian distributions, L√©vy stable distributions are characterized by a stability index ќ± вИИ (0 , 2] , a skewness parameter ќ≤ вИИ [вИТ1, 1] , a scale parameter ќ≥ > 0, and a location parameter ќі вИИ R, allowing them to model varying degrees of tail heaviness and asymmetry. However, the direct application of stable distributions in deep learning is hindered by the lack of closed-form probability density functions [ 20 , 21 ]. We overcome this obstacle by operating in the spectral domain: since stable distributions admit known characteristic functions (CFs), we train DeepL√©vy by matching the predicted CF to the empirical CF of the targets [ 15 , 14 ]. To address the numerical challenges of CF-based learning, in-cluding the rapid decay of the signal-to-noise ratio at high frequencies, we introduce a scale-adaptive frequency weighting scheme and use the continuous S0-parameterization for gradient stability. In summary, our contributions are as follows: вАҐ We identify that standard finite-variance assumptions are inadequate for modeling extreme events and propose a novel neural architecture that integrates a mixture of L√©vy stable distributions for multi-horizon forecasting. This is among the first works to effectively combine deep learning with the full parameter space of stable distributions ( ќ±, ќ≤, ќ≥, ќі) for time series forecasting. вАҐ To resolve the numerical instability and gradient explosion issues inherent in stable distri-bution parameterization, we propose a robust optimization strategy involving: (i) a scale-adaptive frequency weighting scheme that dynamically adapts to the predicted distributionвАЩs decay rate, (ii) the continuous S0-parameterization that eliminates discontinuities at ќ± = 1 ,and (iii) constrained parameter projections that maintain mathematical validity throughout training. вАҐ Experimental results demonstrate that DeepL√©vy achieves superior performance in capturing tail risks. Specifically, compared to state-of-the-art probabilistic baselines, our model significantly improves the accuracy of tail estimation on both synthetic heavy-tailed datasets and real-world financial and epidemiological benchmarks. 

2 Related Works 

Deep Probabilistic Forecasting: Deep probabilistic forecasting estimates the full predictive distribu-tion rather than point predictions alone [ 9]. Standard parametric approaches, including RNN-based models like DeepAR [ 30 ], DeepVAR [ 29 ], and DSSM [ 24 ], alongside recent Transformer-based or causal architectures such as ProbTransformer [ 33 ], MTSNet [ 41 ], and STOAT [ 42 ], typically combine neural sequence encoders with parametric likelihood heads. While these methods have explored a spectrum of distributions ranging from Gaussian and Laplace to StudentвАЩs t, they fundamentally rely on the existence of a closed-form probability density function (PDF) to perform exact maximum likelihood estimation (MLE). This dependence constitutes a prohibitive barrier for modeling L√©vy stable distributions, which generally lack analytical PDFs. Although recent studies have proposed 2approximating the cumulative distribution function (CDF) of L√©vy processes using mixtures of logistic distributions for option pricing simulations [16], such methods are tailored for Monte Carlo generation rather than direct multi-horizon forecasting. Consequently, effective modeling of heavy-tailed dynamics in sequence forecasting necessitates alternative estimation strategies beyond classical MLE and CDF approximations. 

Heavy-Tailed Modeling and L√©vy Assumption: To address the limitations of Gaussian assumptions in modeling extreme events, parametric approaches often employ the StudentвАЩs t distribution due to its tractable density [ 17 ]. However, its reliance on symmetric tails and a single degrees-of-freedom parameter restricts its ability to capture the directional asymmetry and time-varying tail dynamics characteristic of financial crashes or epidemic outbreaks. L√©vy distributions [ 35 ] offer a rigorous generalization, employing separate parameters for tail heaviness and skewness to naturally model infinite variance and asymmetric extremes. Despite their theoretical advantages, estimating stable parameters is notoriously difficult due to the lack of closed-form PDFs. Classical characteristic function estimators [ 15 ] and Bayesian MCMC approaches [ 4] are often computationally prohibitive for high-dimensional gradient-based learning. While recent neural approaches like Xu and Darve [39] also exploit characteristic functions to sidestep intractable multivariate L√©vy densities, their objective is inverse modeling: calibrating a L√©vy process to match observed increments under a stationary/incremental likelihood surrogate. DeepL√©vy instead uses CF matching as a forward 

training signal for a sequence-to-sequence forecaster: an encoderвАУdecoder predicts horizon- and context-dependent mixture stable parameters, and the loss compares predicted mixture CFs to single-horizon empirical CF exponentials eiѕД y T +h inside a scale-adaptive frequency weighting scheme. Thus the spectral-domain idea is related, but the problem class (conditional multi-horizon forecasting vs. stationary process calibration), the parameterization (context-varying mixtures), and the optimization template differ substantially.

4 Methodology 

In this section, we present DeepL√©vy, our framework for heavy-tailed probabilistic forecasting. The architecture follows a modular design: (1) a sequence encoder maps historical observations to a latent representation, (2) an autoregressive decoder generates horizon-specific representations, (3) a constrained projection layer transforms these representations into valid mixture parameters, (4) characteristic function matching is performed with scale-adaptive weighting for training, and (5) the Chambers-Mallows-Stuck algorithm generates samples during inference. 4.3 Inference and Sampling 

During inference, the goal is to generate predictive samples from the learned mixture distribution over the entire prediction horizon. Given a new historical sequence x1: T , we first compute the context representation c = EќЄ (x1: T ) and then autoregressively generate samples for each horizon. For each horizon h = 1 , . . . , H : (1) compute the horizon-specific hidden state h(h) using Eq. (7) ;(2) obtain the predicted parameters via the constrained projection; (3) sample a component index 

kвИЧ вИЉ Categorical (ѕА(h)) and generate a sample from that component using the Chambers-Mallows-Stuck (CMS) algorithm [ 5] (see Algorithm 2 for the complete procedure under S0 parameterization); (4) feed the sampled value back to the decoder for the next horizon. The CMS algorithm enables efficient and mathematically rigorous sampling from the heavy-tailed predictive distribution under the S0 parameterization. For probabilistic forecasting, we generate Nsamples independent trajectories by repeating the entire autoregressive sampling process, which provides Monte Carlo estimates of any desired statistics. The complete CMS sampling procedure is detailed in Appendix A.4.

6 Conclusion 

We introduced DeepL√©vy, a neural framework for heavy-tailed probabilistic forecasting that models predictive distributions as mixtures of L√©vy ќ±-stable components. By matching characteristic functions rather than maximizing intractable likelihoods, DeepL√©vy learns context-dependent tail behavior end-to-end via standard gradient descent. Experiments on financial and epidemiological time series demonstrate substantial improvements in tail risk quantification while remaining competitive on overall forecasting accuracy. Our work demonstrates the feasibility of integrating L√©vy stable distributions with deep neural networks for heavy-tailed uncertainty learning. Current limitations include the parametric stable assumption and larger batch size requirements compared to likelihood-based methods.
